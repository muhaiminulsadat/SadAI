## Bob Baxley

**Insight:** A successful design brief provides clear constraints and context while leaving the visual solution entirely to the designer.

**Tactical advice:**
- Focus the brief on the future state you want to create rather than the specific features to build.
- Wait as long as possible to draw pictures to ensure the problem space is fully defined first.

*Source: [35 years of product design wisdom from Apple, Disney, Pinterest, and beyond | Bob Baxley](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=X-83gvgVaWc) @ 01:01:53*

---

**Insight:** Prematurely creating high-fidelity prototypes can anchor a team to a suboptimal solution before they have fully explored the problem space.

**Tactical advice:**
- Wait as long as possible to create a prototype or draw a picture of your idea.
- Prioritize verbal and conceptual alignment on the desired outcome before moving to visual design.

*Source: [35 years of product design wisdom from Apple, Disney, Pinterest, and beyond | Bob Baxley](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=X-83gvgVaWc) @ 01:09:04*

## Jenny Wen

> "We used to go off and make this two-year, five-year, 10-year vision even. Now it becomes a vision that's three to six months out, and isn't necessarily creating this beautiful deck, sometimes just creating a prototype that points people in the right direction."

**Insight:** The speed of AI-driven engineering requires design to shift from static, long-term theoretical planning to short-term functional prototyping and real-time execution support.

**Tactical advice:**
- Replace multi-year vision decks with three-to-six-month directional prototypes.
- Reduce time spent on high-fidelity mocks to prioritize supporting engineers with 'last mile' implementation and polish.
- Test functional prototypes with actual models to account for the non-deterministic nature of AI behavior.

*Source: Jenny Wen @ 00:04:59*

## Lenny Rachitsky

**Insight:** Continuously returning to the defined problem and success metrics serves as a filter to reject features that don't directly serve the project's goal.

**Tactical advice:**
- Keep coming back to the original problem statement during every phase of development.
- Evaluate every proposed feature by asking if it increases the likelihood of hitting your defined success metric.
- Cut any scope that does not directly contribute to solving the core problem.

> "Instead of suggesting something will be quick, ask, “What would need to be true for this to be doable in the next x days?” It’s totally OK to suggest something will be quick occasionally, but if you’re finding that the majority of your asks should take “just” a few hours, you’re probably annoying the sh*t out of your team."

## Vijay

> "And if you're shipping features that quick leads, you don't have time to stop and think, where does this go, and how does this fit into our overall system architecture? And what started to happen was that we were hitting diminishing returns with some of these features. And not considering the holistic design and consistency meant the reach of every feature was low."

**Insight:** High-velocity feature shipping eventually hits a wall of diminishing returns unless the product is anchored by a consistent system architecture and design building blocks.

**Tactical advice:**
- Identify the minimal number of core building blocks required for your product's system architecture.
- Pivot to a design-led initiative to ensure consistency once high-velocity shipping begins to fragment the user experience.
- Establish design as a key differentiator to improve feature discoverability and reach across the platform.
